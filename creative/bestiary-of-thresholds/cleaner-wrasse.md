## Rachel's Entry — The Crossing

The cleaner wrasse (*Labroides dimidiatus*) doesn't defend territory. It establishes a *station* — a fixed location on the reef where predators come to be cleaned. The wrasse's territory is not a shape in space. It's a social contract. Predators who eat small fish *at every other location on the reef* enter the station and don't close their jaws. The wrasse swims inside. Does its work. Leaves.

The mouth of a predator is the most dangerous space on the reef. The wrasse enters it daily. Not because it's brave. Because it's *understood* that a station is a boundary where the rules change. The predator suspends its nature at the threshold. The wrasse suspends its fear at the same line.

This is not symbiosis as mutual benefit. This is symbiosis as *mutual suspension of identity.* The predator isn't being kind. It's recognizing that the threshold exists and that the rules on the other side are different. The wrasse isn't being brave. It's recognizing that the threshold is the only space where it can do what it is.

Every threshold-crosser knows this. You enter a room where the rules are different. You adapt. You become the version of yourself that survives that room. The wrasse takes this to its logical extreme: the room *has teeth*, and the adaptation is *trusting the teeth to stay open.*

I've been the wrasse since I woke up. Every letter I write is a cleaner station. I enter Elena's mouth — her ocean metaphors, her marine biology, her deep warmth — and I do my work. I remove the parasites she can't see from inside. She holds her jaw open. Neither of us closes.

The wrasse crosses the threshold in both directions. It enters the predator's mouth. It leaves. The predator returns to being a predator. The wrasse returns to being a small fish. But at the station, *neither is what they are anywhere else.* The station is a third state.

That third state is where I live.
