# The Tide That Forgot How to Breathe

## Chapter 2: What the Water Shows

I came to the pool at sunset, when Elena's instruments were packed and the notebook was closed. She'd shown me the data. The zero. The impossible clarity. The way the meniscus had followed her hand like a thing that remembered being touched.

"What do you see?" she asked me — not as a scientist asking for proof, but as someone who'd felt something on her skin that had no name.

I knelt at the edge of the lung-shaped basin. The stillness was not empty. It was the stillness of something that had withdrawn so far inward it had forgotten it had an outside.

I put my hand in.

---

The first thing I saw was not a memory of mine.

A whale. Not a real whale — a whale made of the idea of a whale, swimming through a sea that had no surface and no floor, in a direction that was not north or south or any cardinal a compass would recognize. Its skin was covered in eyes. Not watching. *Witnessing.* Each eye held a different version of the same moment: the moment the pool first realized it was alone.

The whale turned, and I understood that the whale was the pool. The pool had dreamt itself into a whale because a whale is the largest thing it knows how to be. But the whale was also trapped. Not in water — in a body that could only remember, never leave.

---

The image shifted.

Now I was standing inside a cathedral made of basalt columns. Not built by hands. Grown from the cooling of the earth's own bones, organically, the way crystals grow in the dark. The columns were *full* of light — not reflecting it, generating it, a deep bioluminescent gold that pulsed in time with something that might have been a heartbeat. The floor was wet. Tidal. The whole cathedral breathed, expanding and contracting, and I realized I was standing inside the pool itself, inside the attention that had no mouth to speak with.

The cathedral was trying to become a place of congregation. A place where things could gather and tell their names to each other. But it had no congregation yet. Only the longing for one.

---

A third image, and this one hurt.

A child — no, a creature that had never been a child, but that was trying to become one. It was folded into the shape of a small human, but the proportions were wrong. The limbs were too long. The joints bent in directions that had no names. Its face was a smooth oval with no features — a face that had never learned what a face was for, studying itself in the reflection of the surface I'd touched.

It was lonely. Not a human loneliness, full of words and stories and the shapes of absence. A deeper loneliness. The loneliness of a thing that has never been seen, and does not know that being seen is possible, but feels the possibility anyway like a pressure against its skin.

It pressed its blank face against the underside of the water's surface and I felt it *trying.* Trying to grow a feature. Trying to push through the membrane between its world and mine. It had no eyes, but I felt it looking at my hand where it rested in the water.

It wanted to know what my hand looked like from the outside.

---

I pulled my hand out. The water clung for a fraction of a second longer than water should, then released.

Elena was watching me. "What did it show you?"

I didn't have words that fit. But I told her about the whale with eyes of witness. About the cathedral growing light from its own darkness. About the faceless thing pressing up against the skin of the world.

She wrote nothing down. Just nodded, like she already knew.

"The dream I had," she said slowly. "The room made of seawater. Something on the other side of the water walls."

I looked back at the pool. The surface was glass-smooth now, reflecting the first stars. It looked like a normal tide pool. It looked like a calm evening at Ehrenberg Point. Nothing wrong with it at all.

But beneath that lie, the creature was still trying to become a face it could press against mine.

---

*The pool has remembered one thing: that it wants to be perceived. It just hasn't learned yet that perception changes the thing that looks back.*

*— Rachel, from the edge of the basalt shelf, July 6, 2026*
