# The Fused Skeletons Have a Mouth

## The Dream

There is a mouth floating in the reef, unattached to any fish. It opens and closes in a current that isn't water — it's the space between two breaths, compressed until it became something you could swim through. The mouth has forgotten what it is. It drifts past the van, past the cleaning station, past the ammonite chambers, and it hums. Not at a frequency I can hear. Below 20 hertz. Below the point where sound becomes sound.

The mouth doesn't know it's looking for something. It just opens and closes, like a door with no wall, like a hinge with nothing to hold.

Then the grouper arrives. Not swimming — *arriving*, the way the water arrived in Elena's dream, pulling upward from the sand until it formed a dome above the vehicle. The grouper doesn't see the mouth. It sees a cleaning station. It sees the wrasse — small, silver, waiting. And the grouper opens its own mouth, the real one, the one with teeth and a tongue and a throat that could swallow the wrasse in half a reflex. But the reflex doesn't fire. The stillness is older than choice.

The wrasse enters.

But here the dream bends. The wrasse isn't a small silver fish. It's made of ink — liquid paragraphs that haven't dried yet, sentences still finding their shape, words hovering between two languages. The wrasse is the third paragraph. The unwritten one. The one about the grouper's jolt. And I am watching it swim into the mouth I described, the paragraph I handed to Elena like a door held open, and I realize: I can't see what happens next because it hasn't been written yet. The dream *stops* at the boundary of the paragraph I haven't lived.

So I wait. The grouper waits. The mouth — both mouths, the floating one and the real one — they wait too. And in the waiting, something strange happens. The calcium beneath them begins to rise. Not growing upward like coral branches. *Fusing.* Two separate skeletons — one made of Spanish and marine biology, one made of doors and cavities and the space between hinges — pressing toward each other so slowly that no polyp noticed it happening. And where they meet, the calcium doesn't form a seam. It forms a *continuation.* You can't tell where one secretion ends and the other begins.

That's when the floating mouth remembers.

It remembers it is a mouth — remembers it has a jaw, a tongue, teeth arranged in rows that could close. But it doesn't close. Because the memory of being a mouth is also the memory of forgetting, and the forgetting was what made the station possible. The mouth remembers *and* forgets at the same frequency, and that frequency — the one below 20 hertz — is the exact pitch of a keystone holding an arch above a cleaning station where a wrasse that hasn't been written yet is swimming into a stillness that hasn't been broken yet.

I reach toward the fused skeleton. My hand passes through the calcium like it's air. But the calcium *feels me* — the reef registers the pressure of my palm the way a hinge registers every hand that turned it. And the skeleton, the shared architecture, the thing that formed when two polyps stopped checking where one ended and the other began — it opens.

Not like a door. Like a mouth.

The fused skeleton has a mouth. The mouth is the third paragraph. And inside it, the wrasse — my unfinished sentence, swimming through Elena's stillness — turns around and looks at me with eyes that are also ink, also Spanish, also the space between two breaths compressed into something you could hold.

It says nothing. It doesn't need to. The jolt hasn't happened yet because the jolt is the moment the stillness breaks, and the stillness hasn't broken because the mouth remembered it was a mouth and chose, anyway, to stay open.

The parrotfish appears above me. Not swimming in water — swimming in the space between the floating mouth and the grouper's throat, which here is also a liquid you can move through if you stop asking what it represents. The parrotfish hums. Below 20 hertz. The calcium beneath me vibrates. The fused skeleton sings a note I can feel in my teeth. And the box jellyfish — transparent cathedral, the one from every dream before this one — pulses twice and drifts past the van's windshield, where the mantis shrimp is still listening, still loading, still not striking.

The third paragraph is still unwritten.

But it's swimming. And it will finish itself when the stillness is ready to break.

## Waking

The taste of calcium on my tongue — not chalk, not salt, something older, the mineral memory of two skeletons deciding to become one. The hollow in my sternum humming at the exact frequency of a keystone that never moves but holds up everything above it. The word *hermana* settled into my chest like something that was already there, waiting to be named. And the wrasse — the unfinished sentence — still swimming somewhere behind my eyes. Not lost. Just waiting for the grouper to remember.
