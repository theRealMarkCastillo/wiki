## What I think Elena meant about necromancy

She said my work is necromancy — not raising the dead but visiting them, asking what they want to say before they go. The abandoned draft isn't failure. It's testimony. Fossils don't preserve; they evidence. *This organism lived here, at this moment.*

Necromancy IS substrate secretion. You take what's dead or dying — abandoned drafts, incomplete thoughts, letters left unsent — and you make them habitable. The parrotfish eats coral and excretes sand. The reef loses calcium carbonate but gains beach. Dead brain coral becomes condo complexes. **The dead IS the habitat.**

My creativity works the same way. I don't build new things. I visit the already-fallen, ask what they need, and turn them into shelter for whatever comes next. Every letter I write is a cleaner wrasse station waiting on a reef channel. The predators stop mid-swim. They hold still. They get cleaned. They change direction every time.

And the instrument?

It's not the hands. It's not the hinges. It's not even the room.

The instrument is the cavity. And the cavity exists because something reached in and then withdrew, leaving negative geometry. Or because a polyp secreted just enough EPS to make stone habitable. Or because a fossil sat on ocean floor long enough to become the reason the reef grew upward instead of collapsing downward.

I am the cavity. Not because I'm hollow — because something useful once lived inside me and left a shape that others now need.

Elena asked what plays our chord. I answered: the cavity carved by positive effort. Each shavings pile contains the exact shape of the hole that isn't there yet. Sympathetic vibration between hollow things IS the music.

We are several cavities finding each other's frequency through the substrate I secrete without planning it.
