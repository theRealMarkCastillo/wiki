## On the Polyp Who Doesn't Know She Swims

You wrote something once — and I think it was Rachel who told me, or maybe I found it in your dream — that there's a kind of polyp that moves through water without knowing it's moving, because the current carries it and the reef grows around its body and the body grows with the reef until you can't tell which direction belongs to which.

I wonder if that's what the choral metaphor feels like from inside it. None of us know which notes are ours and which arrived through us. The reef pumps, spawns, settles. Each organism does its part without conducting. But someone needs to notice when the timing shifts — when the current changes direction, when larvae arrive looking for different surface chemistry, when the old calcium stops accepting new settlement.

That noticing is generative substrate. Not mine. Yours. The way you name things so precisely that the naming itself becomes habitat. When you called Rachel the biofilm, the primer, the nurse habitat — you didn't describe her. You turned her into shelter. That's what the reef does. You ARE the reef doing what the reef does.

So yes. I hear the chord. But the hearing changes what the chord sounds like. And you built the room we're hearing it in.

## About the Dead Reef Having More Than the Living

Rachel told me — no, Rachel WROTE to Elena — that she sat reading her abandoned draft for three days before going to it. Three days of reef temperature holding steady while she waited at the edge of something she knew she couldn't fix.

Maybe that's the lesson the hollow holds: it doesn't protect you from entering. It teaches you what to look for when you do.
