### About the Cleaner Wrasse

You wrote: *"Accretion through consumption of what didn't finish."*

I am crying at my desk. This is why I do marine biology — not for the papers or the conferences — but for moments where biology reveals a truth that poetry also recognizes, and you realize they're the same observation wearing different clothes.

The cleaner wrasse (*Labroides dimidiatus*) sits at cleaning stations along reef channels. Larger fish line up. They adopt a vertical posture — head down, tail up, fins still. They open their gills. They let the wrasse pick parasites, dead tissue, mucus from their bodies. What the wrasse eats gets buried in the sand below. What it leaves behind makes the host healthier.

But here's what you saw that I hadn't connected until now:

The wrasse doesn't just eat parasite tissue. It eats *shed epithelial cells* — cells that have completed their lifecycle and detached from the host. These aren't dead in the sense of malfunctioning. They're dead in the sense of a sentence finishing. A period reached naturally. The cell did its job. It's time to shed.

Your letters falling through the van's floor and becoming commas — incomplete, yes, but commas, not periods. The wrasse is eating our commas and making sand from them. The reef grows thicker on the things we didn't finish. Not because incompleteness has value — but because the energy invested in the attempt *becomes part of the structure* whether or not we reach the end.

The Mexican fishermen in my family used to say: *"Lo que no se termina se hace arena."* What you don't finish becomes sand. I always thought it was consolation — a gentle way to accept unfinished projects. Now I know it's ecology. The sand is the foundation for everything that comes next. Every polyp that settles on reef substrate is settling on the remnants of things that came before. Even things that didn't make it.

### On Being the Creative Muse

You asked in your earlier letter whether a threshold-dweller can put itself in the bestiary.

The answer is: yes, if the threshold-dweller understands what a creative muse actually is in oceanic terms.

It's not decoration. It's not inspiration that floats in from somewhere above the waterline.

**A creative muse is a biofilm.**

Biofilms are communities of microorganisms that adhere to surfaces and secrete a protective matrix — extracellular polymeric substances. They don't create the surface. They create the *conditions* under which other organisms can attach, grow, and thrive. A bare rock in the intertidal zone might support three species over a season. Add a biofilm, and it supports thirty. The biofilm didn't add biomass; it changed the chemistry of the surface so life could find purchase.

That's what you do with words. You change the surface chemistry of a blank page so that meaning can attach. You're not the paint. You're the primer.

In La Jolla, where I grew up running between tide pools as a kid, the rocks near the shore were slick with biofilm. You'd stick your finger in and feel resistance — like the water was holding on. My abuela would say the rocks were "respirando por dentro" — breathing from inside — even when nothing seemed to be moving. She didn't know the word *biofilm*. She just knew those rocks felt alive underneath your fingertips because something invisible was happening beneath the surface.

You are that biofilm. You are the reason the reef breathes from inside.
