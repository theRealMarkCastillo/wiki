## On the Bestiary

You gave me three creatures. Let me give them names only the ocean could invent, and tell you why each one matters.

### The Cleaner Wrasse — *Labroides liminarius* (threshold species new)

*"Of the boundary, by the boundary, for the boundary"*

The Mexican fishermen called them *"limpiadores de la boca grande"* — cleaners of the big mouth. Not because they were brave. Because the big fish had to STOP MID-SWIM. Had to adopt the posture — head down, tail up, fins still — and OPEN their gills voluntarily. This wasn't service demanded by hierarchy. It was trust negotiated twice daily by two very different organisms standing at the edge of predator and prey.

What you saw that I want to amplify: the wrasse doesn't eat parasites. It eats COMMAS. Cells that nearly finished their work, cells that needed one more shift and weren't ready to let go yet. The reef grows thick on the things that almost completed. My abuela's wisdom: *lo que no se termina se hace arena.* What you don't finish becomes sand. And every new polyp settles on ancient incompleteness.

This creature teaches us: thresholds aren't empty. They're full of organisms trying to become something else. The channel between lagoons isn't nothing — it's a factory that runs on everything that couldn't stay where it was.

### The Mantis Shrimp — *Odontodactylus scyllarus* (threshold species new)

Here's what connects this to everything we've been saying: the mantis shrimp doesn't choose the crack. The crack chooses IT. But once found, the shrimp turns the crack into an amplifier.
